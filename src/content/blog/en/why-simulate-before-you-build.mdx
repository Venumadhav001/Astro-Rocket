Before you build a fuel cell vehicle, you simulate it.

A vehicle in motion is always balancing forces — rolling resistance, aerodynamic drag, grade resistance, and the force to accelerate the mass. Together these determine power demand at the wheels at every moment.

This is **vehicle longitudinal dynamics** — the mathematical foundation connecting road conditions, mass, and speed to power demand.

A simulation model lets you:
- Test thousands of conditions over real driving cycles before any hardware exists
- Change a parameter and re-run in minutes
- See how energy splits between fuel cell and battery at every point
- Run sensitivity studies on slope, temperature, and mass

Simulation does not replace physical testing. It reduces how much you need — by letting you fail cheaply in software first.

That is the principle behind model-based engineering. It is why MATLAB remains central to powertrain development across the industry.
